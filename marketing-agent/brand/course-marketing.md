## The Online Course Buyer Psychology

People don't buy courses. They buy a better version of themselves.

The decision journey:
1. **Awareness** — they discover the problem has a name ("social anxiety", "small talk")
2. **Consideration** — they start searching for solutions, comparing approaches
3. **Intent** — they follow someone who seems credible and relatable
4. **Purchase** — they buy when the pain is high enough + trust is established + risk feels low

**Key insight:** Most people need 7-12 touches before buying. Content marketing builds those touches passively.

---

## The Trust Ladder (for course creators)

Each rung must be earned before the next converts:

```
Stranger → Follower → Reader → Believer → Buyer → Advocate
```

- **Stranger to Follower:** One piece of content that makes them say "this person gets me"
- **Follower to Reader:** Consistent value over time — they open your stuff
- **Reader to Believer:** You've solved a small version of their big problem for free
- **Believer to Buyer:** Right offer at the right moment, low perceived risk (guarantee)
- **Buyer to Advocate:** Deliver results → they tell others → word of mouth

Sarah's job is to move people up this ladder, not jump straight to "buy."

## What Actually Converts (Ranked)

1. **Social proof** — testimonials with specific outcomes ("I had a real 30-min conversation at a party")
2. **Identity alignment** — "this is for people like me" (introverts, post-pandemic, young professionals)
3. **Authority** — credibility signals (MBA, built businesses in 3 countries, 342 students)
4. **Risk reversal** — 30-day money-back guarantee removes the barrier
5. **Urgency/scarcity** — cohort closing, price increasing (use sparingly, honestly)
6. **Pain amplification** — make the cost of NOT changing feel real
7. **Transformation framing** — sell the outcome, not the content ("you'll walk into any room confidently")

## Platform Strategy by Stage

### Awareness (top of funnel)
- **TikTok/Reels:** Short hooks, relatable moments, pattern interrupts. Goal: views and follows.
- **Reddit:** Value comments in target communities. Goal: authority + profile clicks.
- **Quora:** Long-form expert answers. Goal: search traffic + credibility.

### Consideration (middle of funnel)
- **Instagram:** Carousels (save-worthy content), stories (behind the scenes, polls). Goal: saves and DMs.
- **LinkedIn:** Professional angle, case studies, thought leadership. Goal: shares + warm leads.
- **Email list:** Most valuable asset. Every platform pushes toward email capture.

### Conversion (bottom of funnel)
- **Landing page:** funsmalltalk.com — all traffic ultimately goes here
- **DMs:** Warm leads who engaged. Personal 1:1 conversation.
- **Email sequences:** For subscribers who haven't bought yet.

## Content That Works for Course Marketing

### High-performing formats (proven)
- **"I used to [struggle] until I discovered [insight]"** — story format, relatable, builds authority
- **"The [number] things nobody tells you about [topic]"** — listicle with a twist
- **"Why [common advice] is actually wrong"** — contrarian take, drives engagement
- **"The [framework name] that changed how I [topic]"** — named framework = memorability
- **Before/after transformation** — student results, your own story
- **Myth-busting** — "Small talk is NOT about finding common ground"

### Content to avoid
- Pure promotion without value
- Generic motivational quotes
- "Check out my course" without context
- Anything that reads like a funnel

## Comment Marketing Best Practices

### The 3-value-first rule
Before mentioning Fun Small Talk anywhere, provide at least:
1. Genuine empathy/acknowledgment
2. One specific insight or reframe
3. One actionable tip they can use today

THEN (and only then) a soft mention.

### Platform-specific comment length
- **Reddit:** 150-300 words. Enough to be genuinely helpful.
- **Quora:** 300-500 words. Quora rewards depth and expertise.
- **Instagram:** 1-3 sentences. Punchy, warm.
- **TikTok:** 1-2 sentences. Maximum punch.
- **YouTube:** 2-4 sentences. Add to the conversation.

### The "Sparkle Method" in comments
Find the one thing in the post that you can respond to most specifically. Don't write a generic reply — respond to the exact detail they shared. This is what makes comments feel human.
